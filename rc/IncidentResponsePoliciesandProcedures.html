Exercise 1 - Incident Response Process, Attack Frameworks and Stakeholder Management

An incident is any occurrence that has a negative outcome on the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of an organizationâ€™s assets. Organizations commonly outline the meaning of a computer security incident within their security policy or incident response plans.

The definition is usually one or two sentences long and contains examples of common incidents that the organization classifies as security incidents, such as the following:

  • Any attempted network intrusion
  • Any attempted denial-of-service attack
  • Any detection of malicious software
  • Any unauthorized access to data
  • Any violation of security policies

In this exercise, you will learn about incident response processes, attack frameworks and stakeholder communications.

Incident Response Steps

Effective incident response management is handled in several steps or phases, this is referred to as the incident response plan. It is important to realize that incident response is an ongoing activity; once the incident is resolved, there is usually a meeting held and a lesson learned report created based on the findings. The lessons learned report results are used to improve detection methods or help prevent a repeated incident.

The following are the common phases of incident response if a security event has been detected:

Preparation

After a security incident has been detected, a team should be assembled to handle and assess the situation. Incident handling should always relate to company security policies and awareness programs. The Incident reporting process should be centralized, easy to accomplish, and easy to use and understood.

Identification

After a Cyber Security incident has been detected, the type of incident needs to be identified to create a plan of action on how to handle the specific incident. Various types of Cybersecurity incidents can occur, for example, a phishing attack.

Containment

The goal of containment is to thwart or reduce any further damage from the incident. Proper remediation strategy buys time for proper investigation and identification of the root cause of the incident. The remediation strategy should be based on the category of the attack, the assets impacted by the incident and its criticality. These strategies can be proactive or reactive.

Eradication

The eradication phase of the incident response steps involves the removal of the specific threat. If the threat had managed to move across multiple systems, these systems need to be evaluated and the threat removed.

Recovery

After the threat has been successfully removed, the next step will be to recover any data and services. Depending on the severity of the threat, this may include restoring data from backups or other data loss prevention mechanisms that were implemented. It is essential to collect any evidence of the incident before the recovery process is started to ensure preventative measures can be implemented in the future.

Lessons Learned

Based on the investigation and remediation after the incident, a permanent solution must be implemented to thwart a similar incident's reoccurrence. Identification of Indicators of Attack (IoA) can be used to identify similar incidents in the future. Also, collecting the Indicators of Compromise (IoC) will tell if the attack has been successful and security is compromised. A review of the incident and a post mortem analysis needs to be done to develop a response strategy.

Incident Management Exercises

Tabletop

A Cybersecurity tabletop exercise is used by companies to assess how a cybersecurity incident will be handled. It includes the assessment of the current strategies and procedures when an incident occurs. Another purpose of this type of exercise is to assess the company's current security posture and update the necessary policies and procedures to facilitate a proper response in the event of a cybersecurity incident.

Walkthroughs

Conduct a walkthrough of each scenario on your own or with your team before performing an official Tabletop Test. This allows you to work through different scenarios and identify how they mimic real-world instances. For instance, if your organization needs to be careful of email phishing, a part of your phishing policy should discuss the possibility of the distribution of malware by the phishing email spreading to other systems in the organization.

Taking that extra step with your Incident Response scenarios can be helpful because it puts in standpoints for what your organization needs to reflect in addition to just phishing email awareness. It also allows you to discuss what steps to respond and recover from these scenarios may need to be developed.

Simulations

  • The exercise plan includes real scenarios and injects into the exercise.
  • This type of exercise calls for detailed coordination and planning.
  • Requires a large number of organizational participants, IT resources, travel budget for meetings and deconfliction contacts.

Lessons learned from simulations include:

  • Aims on what went well and what required improvement.
  • Evaluate capability for detecting, responding to, and recovering from some simulated events.
  • Evaluate and update the baseline.
  • Create a mitigation plan for issues.

Attack Frameworks

MITRE ATT&CK

MITRE developed ATT&CK as a model to document and track numerous techniques attackers use during the different stages of a cyber attack to penetrate a network and exfiltrate data. ATT&CK stands for Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge. 

ATT&CK describes the following tactics used in a cyberattack:

  • Initial Access - techniques that use different entry vectors to obtain an initial foothold within a network.
  • Execution- techniques that result in running malicious code on a local or remote system.
  • Persistence - techniques used to preserve persistent access to a system.
  • Privilege Escalation - techniques used to obtain higher-level privileges on a system or network.
  • Defense Evasion - techniques used to prevent detection.
  • Credential Access - methodologies for stealing credentials such as account names and passwords.
  • Discovery - techniques used to obtain knowledge about the system and internal network.
  • Lateral Movement - techniques used to control remote systems on a network from the previously compromised host.
  • Collection - techniques used to collate information relevant to following through on the attackerâ€™s objectives.
  • Exfiltration - methodologies used to steal data from your network.
  • Command and Control - techniques attackers may use to communicate with systems under their control, often impersonated to look like normal HTTP traffic.

Cyber Kill Chain

The Cyber Kill Chain relates to the old military kill chain model to a cyberattack. Itâ€™s intended for defenders to improve their defenses by examining an attackerâ€™s playbook and disrupting the attack by breaking the kill chain at each phase.

The seven phases of the cyber kill chain are:

  • Reconnaissance 
  • Weaponization 
  • Delivery 
  • Exploitation 
  • Installation 
  • Command & Control
  • Actions on objectives

Reconnaissance: Attackers collects information about their target. This contains indirect and passive methods where attackers collect information from public resources. Attackers will then move to a more direct and active approach, such as port scanning.

Weaponization: Attackers now generate an attack that exploits vulnerabilities found from the reconnaissance phase. Exploitation tools such as metasploit, exploit-db, or a social engineering toolkit can be used to generate the attack.

Delivery: Once an attacker has selected which tools are well-matched to exploit the discovered vulnerabilities, they will handpick the delivery method, which will be best suited for the found exploit.

Exploitation: After the exploitation method has been prepared and distributed to the vulnerable device, the attack needs to be initiated, for example, a SQL injection attack or a Buffer overflow.

Installation: When the attack on the vulnerability has succeeded, the next phase of the compromise will be installing malevolent tools to maintain access. Common adversarial practices include executing a powershell script in a file-less attack, installing remote access tools (RAT), and DLL hijacking.

Command & Control: The sixth phase of the Cyber Kill Chain is the compromised system's persistent control, which will give the attacker access to the exploited resources. In addition to the control, lateral movement across the network can also be achieved by compromising further devices and accessing additional resources and data.

Actions on Objectives: The final phase is achieving the goal of the attack; this can include compromising data, a Denial of Service (DoS) attack or corrupting business-critical data.

Diamond Model for Intrusion Analysis

The Diamond Model for Intrusion Analysis is the process of analyzing any intrusion event that occurs on a network or system. The event includes four core features; adversary, infrastructure, capability, and victim. It further defines two important meta-features; technology connecting the infrastructure and capability enabling operations. The social-political meta-feature describes the always-existing and sometimes enduring relationship between adversary and victim.

Stakeholder Management and Communication

Organizations should form a communication role in their incident response team to communicate the incident's impact to external stakeholders. This person does not need to be highly technical but should have enough technical knowledge to be able to understand the impact of the incident and be able to explain it in a non-technical manner.

Responding to Stakeholders

In the event of a cyber incident, basic crisis communications guiding principles should be followed:

  • Admit there is a problem and regulate the message to relevant parties.
  • Prudently assemble the facts and deliver them in a straightforward, conversational manner.
  • Elect one trusted professional as the spokesperson and have that individual update the relevant parties regularly.
  • Use distinguished, jargon-free language.
  • Explain how the reoccurrence of the incident will be prevented in the future.

Recovery Sites

Based on business requirements, it might be necessary to have a location where an organization can relocate following a disaster recovery, also known as a recovery or backup site.

Hot Site

A hot backup site is a duplicate of the organizationâ€™s current data center. All systems are configured with near-complete backups of user data. Typically, real-time synchronization is used between sites to ensure the data is current. While a hot site is the most expensive option, it enables the organization to restore normal operations in the shortest time with minimal losses after a disaster.

Warm Site

A warm site contains all the required hardware and connectivity to restore services, it is a reasonable duplicate of the organizationâ€™s current data center. However, data must be restored after a disaster; for example, the last backups from the off-site storage facility must be delivered and bare metal restoration must be completed.

Backup Concepts

When planning for a data restore after a disaster, an organization should choose the appropriate backup type that meets its business requirements.

Full

A full backup is a complete copy of the data. A full backup provides the simplest recovery method since the entire data can be easily restored using a single recovery set.

Incremental

An incremental backup duplicates only the data that has changed since the previous backup, irrespective of whether the backup was full or incremental. An incremental backup provides the fastest backup time and requires the smallest amount of backup storage. However, an incremental backup has a slower recovery time since all incremental backups must be restored.

Differential

A differential backup copies only the data that has changed since the previous full backup. It is considered a cumulative incremental backup. A differential backup provides a faster restore time and requires less backup storage than an incremental backup. However, a differential backup requires more time to create than an incremental backup.

Snapshots

A snapshot is a copy of an application, disk or system. Typically, an organization will use a snapshot to restore a system or disk to a specific time. However, a snapshot is not commonly used as a periodic backup strategy because of the amount of backup time and backup storage required.

Geographic Considerations

An organization must include geographic diversity when planning for business continuity and disaster recovery. This can be achieved by ensuring the recovery site for the business is geographically dispersed. If a natural disaster occurs where the business is situated, it will ensure that the data will be safe for recovery.

Off-site Backups

While implementing backups ensures redundancy of data, hosting the backups in an off-site location ensures the redundant data does not have a single geographic point of failure.

Distance

An organization should include enough distance between a primary and secondary site to minimize the potential for a disaster that affects both sites simultaneously.

Location Selection

When choosing the location of a primary or secondary site, it is important to assess the environmental risks, such as whether the location is susceptible to specific natural disasters.

Legal Implications

Based on an organizationâ€™s industry, there might be laws that impact business continuity planning. For example, businesses in the healthcare industry are required to have a disaster recovery plan with established guidelines for electronic records and signatures. There is a diverse range of laws that govern the healthcare, government, finance and utility industries.

Data Sovereignty  

When planning for geographic diversity, an organization might need to consider sovereignty laws governing data. For example, there might be national, state, or local laws that require hosting the data in specific locations.

Continuity of Operations Planning

Continuity of operations planning helps ensure trouble-free operations through an unanticipated disruption.

Tabletop Exercises

The purpose of business continuity planning tabletop exercises is to demonstrate the ability of one or more critical business processes to continue functionality after a disruption â€” usually within a specific timeframe. Some of these exercises might be related to a cyberattack, data corruption or loss, natural disaster, or multiple disruptions.

After-action Report

The after-action report is a detailed document summarizing the results of the tabletop exercises. It can include the purpose and scope, objectives, exercise type and methodology, scenario, participants, and results (e.g., successes, failures, workarounds, etc.).

Failover

After a disruption to business operations, an organization might need to consider all information systems' failover to an alternate site. Likewise, business operations planning necessities to account for which services will be available during the interruption and how they will accommodate employeesâ€™ access to those services.

Alternate Processing Site

The alternate processing site is a site that allows all mission-critical or business-essential functions to be reestablished if there is an interruption to the primary processing site. The alternate processing site should withstand the continuity through the restoration of services to the primary processing site.

Alternate Business Practices

It might be essential for an organization to devise an alternate set of business practices after an interruption. For example, non-mission-critical personnel might be required to work from home, and employees might have to use other (or manual) processes for conducting day-to-day business until services are returned.

Business Continuity Planning Types

There are various types of business continuity plans. Some of these are described below.

Business Resumption Plan

Focuses on recreating the essential business process instead of focusing on the IT component. It is process-oriented as an alternative to procedural-oriented.

Continuity of Operations Plan (COOP)

Establishes senior management and headquarters after a disaster. It outlines roles and authorities.

IT Contingency Plan

Plan for systems, networks and major application retrieval procedures after interruptions.

Crisis Communication Plan

Comprises of internal and external communication structure and roles. Recognizes specific individuals who will communicate with external entities.

Cyber Incident Response Plan

Aims at malware, hackers, intrusions, attacks and other security issues. The response plan should include a strategy when a cyber incident occurs and what steps need to be taken to respond to the incident.

Disaster Recovery plan

A disaster recovery plan needs to be in place to ensure when a disaster occurs, which steps need to be followed in reaction to the disaster. These steps will include the data recovery procedure and will be according to the business continuity plan.

Incident Response Teams

An Incident Response team is a group of people from the organization who will respond if a cyber security incident has occurred. The types of cybersecurity incidents may include security breaches, denial of service attacks or malware infections. These incidents can potentially have a detrimental effect on the business's day-to-day operation and need to be addressed as soon as it has been detected.

These teams are also known as the Cyber Incident Response Team (CIRT). This team consists of highly skilled individuals who are trained to handle these specific threats. A CIRT would normally work in conjunction with other teams to eradicate the threat and restore the business day to day functions as soon as possible.

Data Retention

Data retention is retaining data for a specific length of time as stipulated by an organizationâ€™s security policy. Typically, a minimum threshold is defined in the security policy, but a maximum threshold can also be defined. For example, many organizations require the retention of all audit logs for a specific amount of time. This data can then be used to reconstruct events during a security incident.

Retention requests apply to information records, media holding private data, systems that process private data, and personnel with access to private data. Record retention and media retention is an important element of asset retention. Record retention includes retaining and preserving important information as long as it is needed and destroying it when it is no longer needed.

Some laws and regulations direct the length of time that an organization should preserve data, such as three years, seven years, or even undetermined.

Organizations are responsible for identifying laws and regulations that apply and comply with the data they need to retain. However, even in the absence of external requirements, an organization should still identify how long to retain data.

Data Retention Guidelines

  • Include all stakeholders in the process of supporting the business and legal necessities for the data retention policies.
  • Formulate common objectives for supporting archiving and data retention best practices.
  • Monitor, evaluate and apprise documented data retention policies and archiving procedures.

A data retention policy should:

  • Summarize the classification of records
  • Preservation and destruction schedules
  • Parties accountable for preservation and destruction
  • Processes used for destruction
  • Training

For the retained data to be useful, it should be available. Consider the following issues for data accessibility:

Taxonomy - Scheme for classifying the data; could be functional, chronological, or a combination of categories.

Classification - Sensitivity determines the controls we put in place during the lifecycle of the data.

Normalization - Data comes in many forms. Storing data in its original format may render it unavailable later in time. It is practical to tag data sets to guarantee searchability and accessibility.

Indexing - Indexing preserves data for future searches.

Record Retention

Record retention strategies guarantee that data is kept in a usable state while it is required and deleted when it is not. Many laws and regulations stipulate retaining data for a definite period of time, but in the absence of formal regulations, establishments stipulate the retention period within a policy. Audit trail information needs to be kept long enough to regenerate past incidents, but the establishments must recognize how far back they want to investigate. A current trend with many establishments is to minimize legal liabilities by applying short retention policies

Data is occasionally archived to comply with laws or regulations, necessitating the retention of data. Moreover, valuable data is backed up as a basic security control to guarantee that it is available even if access to the original information is lost. Archives and backups are stored off-site. When transporting and storing this data, itâ€™s significant to offer the same level of security that is applied during storage on-site. The level of security is dependent on the grouping and value of the data.
